The poach

Crossing over is a weapon. Don't waste it.

Poaching isn't aggression — it's a read. Paddle face, contact height, partner's position. Get it right, you close the point. Get it wrong, you gift an open court.

Go

Green.

  • Partner trapped back — you're the only one at the line.
  • High floater cross-court to partner's side — reach + angle + power.
  • Pre-signaled (open hand) — partner knows it's coming.
  • Paddle opens cross-court pre-contact — you see the shot first.
  • Move as they contact. Not before. Not after.
Don't

Red.

  • Your side goes wide open and opponent is alert.
  • Partner's in rhythm. Trust them. Don't interrupt.
  • 50/50 putaway. That's a gift, not a poach.
  • Habit, not strategy. Three random poaches frustrate partner.
  • Below the net. Can't finish. Let it go.

Silence loses the point.

Every middle ball gets a call. Two players hesitating for half a second = one ball through the seam.

Before the serve

Plan the poach in advance.

Opportunistic poaching is improvisation. Planned poaching is choreography — you and your partner have already agreed, silently, before the point begins. Three tools.

01 · SIGNALS

Flat hand, fist, or fake.

Net player holds a signal behind their back — only partner sees it.

Partner must acknowledge — a nod, a "got it." Silent signals only land when they're received.

02 · STACKING

Pre-point positioning.

Start same-side to keep your strongest side in the middle. Rule 4.B.7 allows it — use it.

Righty-lefty stacks keep both forehands in the middle. Two-forehand teams stack so one is the designated poacher.

03 · VERBAL

Say it out loud.

Before every serve, lock the plan. Three phrases cover 99% of situations:

"I'm going in front." I will cross after the return — cover the line.
"Stay." No switch. Standard positioning.
"Fake it." I'll show the switch move, then recover — disguise the real one.

Signals must be given before the serve — not during. Both partners acknowledge.

The must-hit-a-winner rule.

When you planned-poach, you and partner end up on the same half of the court. You have to close the point — or at minimum force a weak reply partner can recover off. Planned poaches that become defensive scrambles give up more points than they win.
